You’re Nothing But a Fake!

By all accounts, I was a sweet little girl as a child. I was kind, obedient and (I’m ad-libbing here) an overall delight to be around. I had interesting relationships with my two older brothers. Both relationships were good ones, don’t get me wrong, but there was much more of a give-and-take of jokes and jabs with the older one. He would tease me, I would retaliate in my kind, little girl way. All good.

Occasionally, his well-intentioned joking pushed my buttons just a little too much. On one such occasion (and I cannot remember the particulars, only the outcome), my brother’s teasing infuriated me to the point of extreme retaliation. In my eight or nine year old rage I shot back with the greatest insult I could ever think of.

“You’re A . . . A . . . You’re a Fake!

There, I said it.

Even as the words came out of my mouth, I was taken with regret. It hurt me to say the words as much, or perhaps more, than I thought it would hurt my brother to hear them.

His response?

He nearly rolled on the floor with laughter.

Rather than being hurt to the core (my outraged intention) he found me to be one of the silliest and most amusing events of his day.

Now I was hurt.

I wasn’t being taken seriously in one of my more serious moments.

At the time, I’m guessing I had little or no idea what it was exactly to be a fake — or to condemn someone else to that level of hopeless insignificance and irrelevance.

So, while my brother laughed at me because he understood, better than I did, that my accusation was unfounded and misunderstood, I was left to deal with the darker, uglier side of myself that wanted to reach out and hurt someone I (mostly) loved.

It became a lifelong lesson in maturity for me.

These days, I still think the label “Fake” can produce a devastating blow to the recipient. Writers, musicians, creatives of all sorts, must wrestle with the feeling throughout their lifelong journey.

My brother was never a fake (He may have felt like one at times as a struggling musician, but I never saw it in him).

I, on the other hand, can easily apply the label to myself from time to time — those times when any and all efforts in life can feel too small, too imperfect, too lacking.

Maybe it was a childhood premonition.

I remember a little ditty we used to sing as children when one child would say something mean to another child.

“I’m rubber. You’re glue. Whatever you say bounces off me and sticks to you!”

. . . Yeah, that one sure came home to roost.

In those moments when I’m pretty sure I’m just spinning my wheels in life and fooling no one but myself — feeling like a fake, an impostor, whatever — I seek out the companionship of positive, encouraging souls.

There are actually people who believe in me, who think I can, and do, contribute to my world in myriad ways. I need to keep those folks around — if, for no other reason, than to be a voice of calm and balanced reason when my inner voice is shouting out “Fake!”

The highest calling for each of us is not to be the one who shouts out “Fake!” in defense of our own weak position, but to be the one who can shout out “You’ve Got This!” when the right words can be an inspiration.
